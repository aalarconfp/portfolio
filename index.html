01 / Operational Finance Frameworks
Capability Demonstrations · Finance Systems Design
NOTE

The frameworks below demonstrate structured approaches to recurring operational finance challenges. They are capability demonstrations built from direct professional experience — not representations of client implementations, proprietary data, or confidential work product. No real company data, client identifiers, or fabricated financial metrics are used.

Ref Framework & Operational Rationale
F-01
Treasury & Liquidity Management
13-Week Cash Flow Forecasting Model

Most finance teams discover cash constraints at period-end rather than during the period — because reporting runs on actuals, not forward-looking assumptions. This framework shifts the focus to rolling cash intelligence: structured inflow/outflow assumption logic separating committed cash from projected cash, scenario sensitivity for operating plan deviations, threshold-based liquidity alerts, and a weekly treasury summary designed for CFO review. Includes covenant simulation for debt-service environments.

13-Week Rolling Horizon Assumption-Based Logic Scenario Analysis Liquidity Thresholds Covenant Simulation Treasury Summary
F-02
Controllership & Period-Close
Month-End Close & Reporting Architecture

Month-end close failures rarely stem from accounting errors. They come from coordination failures — tasks distributed across email chains, reconciliations with unclear ownership, and no central view of what is complete versus outstanding. This framework replaces informal close coordination with a structured governance layer: close calendar, reconciliation status tracking, task ownership matrix, and completion KPIs visible in real time to the Controller — without requiring constant manual status updates or close-call meetings.

Close Calendar Recon Status Tracker Ownership Matrix Escalation Thresholds Completion KPIs Multi-Entity
F-03
Fixed Assets & Capital Expenditure
Capex & Fixed Asset Control Framework

Capex overruns are typically discovered at period-end, not during project execution. CIP balances age without clear capitalization triggers. Depreciation schedules drift from policy when the asset register is not maintained continuously. This framework provides the control layer between capex approval and the balance sheet: spend tracking against authorized budgets, CIP aging alerts beyond policy thresholds, capitalization workflow documentation, and a depreciation monitoring layer that connects project completion events to the fixed asset register.

Capex Request Tracking Budget vs. Actual CIP Aging Analysis Capitalization Workflow Depreciation Monitor Asset Lifecycle
F-04
FP&A & Performance Management
Budget vs. Actual Variance Reporting System

Variance reporting stops being useful when it answers "what was off" without explaining "what changed operationally." A number without context is noise, not intelligence. This framework is built around the reporting logic that makes variance analysis actionable: structured assumption documentation per department, period-over-period deviation tracking, waterfall visualization connecting budget to actuals, and an executive summary format that links variances to operational decisions — not just accounting classifications. Designed for management review meetings, not period-end filing.

Multi-Department Budget Automated Actuals Import Assumption Documentation Waterfall Logic OPEX Trend Tracking Executive Package
F-05
Working Capital & Liquidity Operations
Working Capital Intelligence Dashboard

Working capital metrics are frequently calculated but rarely integrated into operational decisions — because AR aging lives in one report, AP scheduling in another, and no one has a unified view of cash conversion position between period-end reports. This framework builds that unified layer: AR-to-cash cycle monitoring, AP scheduling against payment terms, inventory velocity, DSO/DPO trend tracking, and a liquidity position summary designed for weekly finance and operations review. The objective is closing the gap between the balance sheet and the bank account.

AR Aging AP Scheduling DSO · DPO Tracking Cash Conversion Cycle Inventory Turnover Liquidity Position
02 / Finance Operations Philosophy
Principle I
Data integrity before reporting velocity
A report produced quickly on unreliable data is worse than no report at all — because it creates false confidence at the decision layer. Every reporting system starts with the question: what does it take for this number to be trusted every period? That answer determines the architecture, not the output format.
Principle II
Process design before visualization
A dashboard is the last layer of a well-designed process, not a substitute for one. Finance systems built in reverse — starting with the visual and working backward — tend to break under operational load. The mapping work happens before the first formula is written: what is the source, who owns it, what classifies it, who reviews it?
Principle III
Financial control works through clarity, not complexity
Control environments fail when they are too complicated to be understood consistently by the people who operate them. The strongest financial control frameworks are structured enough to scale, simple enough to be audited, and clear enough that variance from the standard is immediately visible — without requiring interpretation.
